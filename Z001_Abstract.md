## **The Elevator Pitch**

I'm treating education as a system and not purely a pedagogical abstraction. For this goal, **this book utilizes cross-domain knowledge to suggest improvements towards education and education systems.** 

Because of this book's breadth, I fully expect any reader to delve further into topics discussed here and supplement knowledge with specialized texts, documents, books, and guides. This is more of a starting point than a deep-dive.

Education is discussed in an operational environment and covers various topics, such as:
- Legal, financial, and human constraints
- Stakeholder/client relationships and management principles
- Preparing a classroom environment and curriculum
- Metrics, math, probability, and statistics
- Methodology from other fields and disciplines to enhance existing education systems

Rather than discuss educational theory in isolation, concepts such as system design, engineering, operational scaling, management, and communications are applied to evaluate classroom and educational operations.

Warnings, case studies, and implementation guidance intentionally draw from multiple domains including engineering, design, management, communication, and finance to better inform how and why decisions are made.

## **"I Have <5 Minutes" Overview**

For those who are OK with 99% of solutions, rather than 99.9% or 99.99%+ of solutions, for many education system problems to solve by next Monday morning, here's the broad summary including some actionable items:
- Your first plan will not survive initial contact. Even the advice, suggestions, and frameworks here may need modifications for your specific situation(s). Expect to modify plans and systems as time marches on.
- Most communications should be clear, concise, and descriptive.
- If a rule, regulation, guideline, etc. is in a system and sounds dumb, there's a good chance that rule was written in blood and has a reason "why" behind it.
- Many classroom issues are solved by three methods:
    1. **Everyone** in an education system enforcing consequences for actions and behaviors (i.e. administrators, parents, educators, staff, students, etc.). 
    2. Limiting classroom sizes.
    3. Allowing students to fail.
- Perfect is the enemy of good.
- If suddenly thrusted into managing a classroom with little to no preparation (and perhaps against your will, who knows), focus first on setting up classroom management and procedures, followed by a syllabus and lesson plans. A safe, secure, and orderly environment is needed *before* students may receive a proper education.
- The first year for just about every professional role people go into, especially for educators, will be hard. That is normal.
- Leadership starts from the top. A failure from above quite often leads into failures below.
- Self-regulation in practice is often difficult for students without compromising their education.
- Don't be an asshole.
- You cannot "build relationships" with people that do not want to have them, despite your best efforts. There is a certain point you'll have to accept failure.
- Prepare for the worst, but assume the best of people and students.
- Standards are a *good* thing, but too many standards bottleneck education while too few standards compromise education.
- Apathy is the death of many things. Education included.
- Education systems require proper *management* of students. If you cannot manage students, you cannot provide them a proper education.
- Having enough resources (i.e. wealth) solves most problems inside *and outside* of education systems, even when considering diminishing returns. It is necessary, but not sufficient, for success.
- Almost all data and information you work with and see is affected by bias.
- You cannot neglect critical thinking and quantitative/qualitative reasoning. Students need to know the "why" and not just the "how" to truly learn about something.
- You can broadly categorize task execution in education through lectures, activities/projects, and tests/review.
- If a teacher/educator/etc., initially plan classrooms as if you're the only educator there and you have no backup to rely on. Don't architect a plan including aides and assistants at first unless *absolutely* necessary.
- Orientation (i.e. passporting information) takes an extremely long time, up to months and *years*, and is critical to success for onboarding professionals. Do not neglect it.
- Technology is dangerous and is *still* dangerous even when handled properly. The same goes for many technologies and advancements in general, such as AI (Artificial Intelligence), electricity, and a crowbar.
- Security should not be sacrificed for the sake of convenience.
- Stamina is often traded for engagement.
- You should never automate a bad process and you need backups in case of emergencies. Education at large is a *messy and highly complicated process* you should not fully automate away.
- Education is improved through incorporating concepts from areas expected and unexpected, such as engineering, architecture, design, communication, etc.
- Scaling an organization, system, business, operation, event, etc. without a proper plan, consideration for the environment, and resource/supports in place and available for use is a recipe for disaster.
- Every person involved in education systems (parents, administrators, educators, students, support staff, etc.) has a role to play in education's success. It doesn't matter how good classroom management is; a failure in any part is a risk towards a system failure as a result.
    - Example 1: One disruptive student can willingly, and knowingly, hinder the education of every other student.
    - Example 2: Administrators can compromise the "authority" of educators managing students and harm education.
    - Example 3: One educator can choose not to follow the same rules as other educators in a system, undermining their own position and other educator's positions, resulting in a risk to compromising education.
    - Example 4: Parents may put pressure on administrators to let students not receive consequences for their actions, increasing the risk they negatively affect that student's, and other student's, education as a result.
